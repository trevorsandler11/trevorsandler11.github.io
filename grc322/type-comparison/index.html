Franklin Gothic vs. Trade Gothic

History of each typeface

Franklin Gothic

Franklin Gothic is an American grotesque sans-serif credited to Morris Fuller Benton and developed for American Type Founders (ATF) in the early 20th century (released across 1902â€“1913 in the United States). It was built for high-impact display typography in advertising and editorial headlines, and later expanded into a broader family with condensed and italic styles. Its bold, pragmatic shapes reflect a period when mass printing, newspapers, and advertising demanded sturdy, legible headline type.

Trade Gothic

Trade Gothic is a mid-century American grotesque designed by Jackson Burke for Linotype, first released in 1948 in the United States and expanded in subsequent years. It was developed for editorial production and newspaper composition, especially useful in tighter columns and condensed settings. Trade Gothic is known for practical utility and slightly irregular, human-feeling shapes compared to later, more systematic neo-grotesques.
